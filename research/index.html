Particle Physics with the ATLAS Detector

Smashing protons to look what is inside.

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is the world's largest and most powerful particle accelerator. It consists of a 27-km ring of superconducting magnets with two pipes where particles (mostly protons and lead ions), grouped in bunches, circulate in opposite directions. Each bunch typically contains one billion of particles. A proton beam is defined as a collection of bunches, separated in distance, forming a train of particles. A number of accelerating structures are installed along the way to boost the energy of the particles in the bunches until they reach 99.999999% of the speed of light. Once accelerated, the particle beams are squeezed closer together and make to collide at one of the interaction points dispatched along the LHC ring. At each collision point, detectors are installed to record particles produced from the proton-proton interaction. The data I analyzed during my PhD was collected by the ATLAS detector, a gigantic cylindrical shaped apparatus located at one of the interaction points.

Recording final state particles.

ATLAS is the largest volume detector ever constructed for a particle collider. It is a 46m long cylinder of diameter 25m which sits in a cavern 100m below ground. Take a virtual walk around the ATLAS cavern! The detector weighs 7'000 tonnes and is made of several layers forming six different detecting subsystems organized concentrically around the collision point. Each of these subsystems was designed to record the trajectory, momentum and energy of a certain kind of particle produced during the collisions. Particles that are easily stopped by the detector material, like electrons, are recorded by the innermost layers of ATLAS, while particles that can travel whole the way through a large amount of material, like muons, are detected in the most extraneous parts. The whole detection system is bathed in a magnetic field which bends the trajectories of particles depending on their momentum and charge.

Recording, distributing and treating massive datasets.

Bunch of protons are made to collide every 25ns, which means more than 600 million interactions take place in the ATLAS detector every second. Among those, only a small fraction contains interesting information. A complex system of on-the-fly selection, the ATLAS trigger, is used to record only the relevant physics events depending on the type of particles produced in the collision, their energy and momentum, and the direction in which they propagate in the detector. Different analysis teams investigating different physics aspects will therefore use different trigger configurations. Selected information is taped on site and sent over a worlwide grid in order to be processed and analyzed. The CERN grid was designed for an optimized big data treatment.

My thesis in detail

A study of diffractive phenomena

During my thesis, I studied events produced in a special kind of interaction dubbed "diffractive". In this case, only one of the smashing protons is dissociating into new particles. The other one interacts softly and is then slightly deflected from its initial trajectory. Such proton is recorded by means of detectors located on the left and right side of ATLAS. ALFA and AFP are the two detectors currently used for diffraction studies in ATLAS. They are situated hundreds of meters away from the interaction point in the forward and backward directions. Those detectors should be located as close as possible from the beam line in order to measure protons with an extremely small deflection angle.

Diffractive interactions are interesting because the probability for them to happen when two highly energetic proton collide, refered as their cross section, is poorly quantified. The analytical model used to calculate all the proton-proton cross sections is limited. The probability for a given interaction to happen for a given collision energy, is still relying on empirical descriptions of the content of the interacting particles. A precise measurement of the cross section of diffractive processes allows to shed light on the reliability of such descriptions for high energy collision experiments. In a way, diffraction allows to test the Standard Model of particles.
